1. Semiconductors
★ Exam Tip: Know the band gap values for all three — semiconductor, conductor, insulator. Very commonly tested.

Types of Semiconductors

  • Pure / Intrinsic Semiconductor — used in PIN diode
  • Doped / Extrinsic Semiconductor — P-type or N-type depending on dopant

Energy Bands

For Semiconductors:
  • Energy Band Gap (Forbidden Energy Gap) = 0.3 eV to 1.5 eV
  • C.B. (Conduction Band) is above V.B. (Valence Band)
  • Types: T (Transistor), R (Rectifier), G (Gate)
For Conductors:
  • Overlapping of bands — C.B. and V.B. overlap
  • Types: T (Transistor), R (Rectifier)
For Insulators:
  • Band gap > 5 eV
  • Only when very high voltage is applied, current passes for very short time
  • V.B. energy levels are filled — cannot support conduction
★ NOTE: 1 eV = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ J
★ NOTE: E_photon = hc / λ

2. Semiconductor Materials (Group Elements)

Group IV Elements

  • C (Carbon), Si (Silicon), Ge (Germanium), Sn (Tin), Pb (Lead)
  • Si and Ge → Proper band gap → Used as semiconductors
  • C, Sn, Pb → Too small band gap / wrong properties → not used

Group III Elements — used for P-type doping

  • B (Boron), Al (Aluminium), Ga (Gallium)
  • 3 outermost electrons — creates a hole when added to Si/Ge lattice
  • Ga is costly; Boron is most commonly used

Group V Elements — used for N-type doping

  • N (Nitrogen), P (Phosphorus), As (Arsenic)
  • 5 outermost electrons — 5th electron becomes free (donor electron)
  • As (Arsenic) is commonly used

3. Transistor & Digital Basics

Transistor

  • Radio communication through air — first done by Marconi
  • Transistor invented by William Shockley (Nobel Prize winner)
  • Used for memory (0 or 1) and digital operations

Logic Gates

  • A and B → OR gate → output O
  • NAND gate = F · NAND
  • NOR gate — output cannot be saved / stored

Circuits: Analog vs Digital

  • A.C. Circuits → Analog Circuits  {Average value = 0}
  • D.C. Circuits → Digital Circuits  {0, 1} — can be saved (Discrete Data)  {Avg ≠ 0}
  • ADC = Analog to Digital Circuit
  • DAC = Digital to Analog Circuit
4. Intrinsic Semiconductor
★ Exam Tip: Know what happens at 0K vs 300K. Holes stay in V.B., electrons go to C.B.

Silicon at 0 K

  • Si atoms form covalent bonds with 4 neighbouring atoms
  • At 0 K, all bonds complete → acts as INSULATOR
  • Ec (Conduction Band) is empty — no electrons available for conduction

Silicon at 300 K (Room Temperature = 27°C)

  • Thermal energy breaks covalent bonds
  • Free electrons move to Conduction Band (C.B.)
  • HOLES remain in Valence Band (V.B.)
  • Semiconductor behaviour — current can now flow

Key Properties

  • Holes = deficiency of electrons (e⁻), remain only in V.B.
  • Electrons (e⁻) can move to C.B.
  • Holes have a (+ve) charge equal to magnitude of electron charge
★ NOTE: In Semiconductor: Conductivity (σ) ∝ Temperature — conductivity INCREASES with temperature (opposite to metals)

Charge Carrier Concentration

  • When a covalent bond breaks → 1 electron + 1 hole created (always a pair)
  • nₑ = nₚ → No. of holes = No. of electrons (in pure/intrinsic semiconductor)
  • If n covalent bonds break → 2n charge carriers are created
  • For intrinsic SC: nₑ = nₚ = nᵢ (intrinsic concentration)
★ NOTE: Mass Action Law is INDEPENDENT of doping concentration:  nₑ × nₚ = nᵢ²
5. Extrinsic (Doped) Semiconductor
★ Exam Tip: P-type = holes majority, N-type = electrons majority. The dopant cannot move — only the carriers can.

P-Type Semiconductor

  • Doped with Group III element (e.g., Boron)
  • Boron has 3 valence electrons → creates a HOLE (vacant site) in crystal lattice
  • Holes and e⁻ can move, but B atom cannot move from its lattice
  • Acceptor energy level (EA) just above VB — holes created easily
  • Majority carriers = HOLES  (nₚ >> nₑ)
  • Minority carriers = electrons

N-Type Semiconductor

  • Doped with Group V element (e.g., Phosphorus)
  • P has 5 valence electrons → 5th electron is LOOSELY CONNECTED → becomes free electron
  • Majority carriers = Electrons  (nₑ >> nₚ)
  • Minority carriers = holes
  • Donor energy level (ED) just below CB — easily emptied at room temperature
★ NOTE: Concentration of Donor atoms ≈ Concentration of electrons (majority). Minority holes are ignored in N-type calculations.

Mass Action Law Application

For P-type:   nₑ = nᵢ² / nₚ = nᵢ² / NA      [minority electrons]
For N-type:   nₚ = nᵢ² / nₑ = nᵢ² / ND      [minority holes]

6. Conductivity & Mobility

Mobility (μ)

  • Mobility is a material property
  • Drift velocity: v = μE  (v = drift velocity, E = electric field)
Unit of μ = (m/s) ÷ (V/m) = m²/(V·s)   or   cm²/(V·s)
★ NOTE: μₑ ≈ 2μₕ — electron mobility is about twice hole mobility

Conductivity Formulas

  • Conductivity depends on charge carrier concentration AND mobility
General (Semiconductors):   σ = nₑ·q·μₑ + nₕ·q·μₕ
                                   ↑ electrons    ↑ holes

Intrinsic SC:               σ = nᵢ·q·(μₑ + μₕ)      [since nₑ = nₕ = nᵢ]

N-Type (Extrinsic):         σ ≈ ND·q·μₑ              [nₕqμₕ neglected — minority]

P-Type (Extrinsic):         σ ≈ NA·q·μₕ              [nₑqμₑ neglected — minority]

Resistivity:                ρ = 1/σ

Current Density (J)

  • J = I/A  (Current density = current per unit area)
  • J = σ·E = E/ρ
  • Since v_d = μE:  J = nqv_d
V = E × l        (voltage = field × length)
J = V / (ρ × l)  = σ × V / l
7. Solved Numerical Problems
★ Exam Tip: Follow the exact steps shown. Show units at every step for full marks.
Q.1 — N-type Semiconductor (Mass Action Law) A semiconductor doped with donor atom concentration ND = 10¹⁸/cm³. Intrinsic concentration nᵢ = 10¹⁰/cm³. Find carrier concentrations. Solution:
  • Given: ND = 10¹⁸/cm³,  nᵢ = 10¹⁰/cm³
  • Since ND >> nᵢ:   nₑ ≈ ND = 10¹⁸/cm³  (majority)
  • Using mass action law:  nₕ = nᵢ²/nₑ = (10¹⁰)²/10¹⁸ = 10²⁰/10¹⁸ = 10²/cm³  (minority)
  • Total charge carriers ≈ ND + 2nₕ ≈ ND ≈ 10¹⁸/cm³
Q.2 — Germanium Intrinsic Semiconductor Pure Ge: nᵢ = 2.5 × 10¹³/cm³, μₕ = 1800 cm²/V·s, μₑ = 3800 cm²/V·s (a) Find Resistivity (ρ) of pure Ge: 
σ = q·nᵢ·(μₑ + μₕ)
σ = 1.6×10⁻¹⁹ × 2.5×10¹³ × (3800 + 1800)
σ = 1.6×10⁻¹⁹ × 2.5×10¹³ × 5600
σ = 22400 × 10⁻⁶ Ω⁻¹cm⁻¹

ρ = 1/σ ≈ 44.6 Ω·cm
(b) Add ND = 4.4 × 10¹²/cm³ (N-type doping). Find ρ: 
σₙ = ND·q·μₑ = 4.4×10¹² × 1.6×10⁻¹⁹ × 3800
σₙ = 26752 × 10⁻⁷ Ω⁻¹cm⁻¹

ρₙ = 1/σₙ = 373.80 Ω·cm
8. Diffusion Current

Definition

  • Diffusion current occurs only in semiconductors (not metals)
  • Caused by concentration gradient — when light/heat falls on SC, carriers move from higher concentration area to lower concentration area
★ NOTE: Direction of drift current and diffusion current may be the same or different — depends on bias conditions.

9. Diode (P-N Junction)
★ Exam Tip: Know forward bias = P to +ve terminal. Si drop = 0.7V, Ge drop = 0.3V. Depletion region direction matters.

Definition

  • P-N Junction Diode: A two-terminal, unidirectional P-N junction device
  • Allows flow of current from P-side to N-side (conventional current direction)
  • It is a PASSIVE element
  • Symbol:  ( I →|→ )
  • General purpose diode: 1N4007

P-N Junction Formation

  • When P and N materials are joined → diffusion occurs at junction (concentration gradient)
  • Holes from P-side combine with electrons from N-side → forms covalent bonds
  • DEPLETION REGION forms at the junction
  • Electric field (E) builds up → opposes further diffusion
  • Barrier potential (V₀) is created
★ NOTE: In depletion region: fixed positive ions on N-side, fixed negative ions on P-side

Barrier Potential Values

MaterialBarrier Potential (V₀)Forward Bias Drop
Silicon (Si)~0.7 V0.7 V
Germanium (Ge)~0.3 V0.3 V

Forward Bias (F.B.)

  • N connected to –ve terminal, P to +ve terminal
  • Applied voltage opposes barrier potential
  • When Vₚ – Vₙ ≥ V₀ → current flows
  • Depletion width decreases → diode acts as short circuit (ideal)
  • Real diode has 0.7 V (Si) or 0.3 V (Ge) drop across it

Reverse Bias (R.B.)

  • N connected to +ve terminal, P to –ve terminal
  • Vₚ < Vₙ → Diode becomes OPEN CIRCUITED
  • Depletion width increases + charge concentration increases
  • Only very small reverse saturation current (IS0) flows — due to minority carriers
10. I-V Characteristics of Diode

Diode Current Equation

I_D = I_S0 · [ exp(V_D / n·V_T) – 1 ]

Where:
  V_D  = Diode voltage = Vₚ – Vₙ
  n    = Ideality factor    (Si: n = 2,  Ge: n = 1)
  V_T  = Thermal voltage = kT(K) / q
  k    = 1.38 × 10⁻²³ J/K   (Boltzmann's constant)
  I_S0 = Reverse saturation current (due to minority carriers)
★ NOTE: At room temperature (300K): V_T ≈ 26 mV

I-V Curve Regions

  • Forward Bias region: Exponential rise in current after V₀ (cut-in voltage)
  • Reverse Bias region: Tiny saturation current IS0 (in μA) — nearly flat
  • Breakdown region: Sudden large reverse current at VBreak

Resistance Types

Static Resistance (DC Resistance) — r_DC 
r_DC = V_D / I_D     (ratio at a specific operating point)
Dynamic Resistance (AC Resistance) — r_ac
  • Also called: small signal resistance or incremental resistance
  • r_ac = dV_D / dI_D  (slope of I-V curve at operating point)
r_ac = n·V_T / I_D
★ NOTE: r_ac range: 1 Ω to 50 Ω  |  r_ac < r_DC always
Average / Chord Resistance 
r_avg = ΔV / ΔI    (slope of chord between two operating points)
11. Effect of Temperature on Diode
★ Exam Tip: Temperature increases → I_S doubles every 10°C. V_D decreases 2.5 mV per °C. Both cause left shift of I-V curve.

Reverse Bias — Effect on Minority Carriers

  • If T ↑ by 10°C → I_S × 2 (reverse saturation current doubles)
  • If T ↑ → left shift of I_D curve
  • Used in fire detectors (temperature sensitive device)

Forward Bias — Effect

  • If T ↑ → V_D ↓ = 2.5 mV/°C (voltage drop decreases with temperature)
  • If T ↑ → left shift of I_D curve (same current at lower applied voltage)

12. Breakdown of Diodes
★ Exam Tip: Avalanche > 6V, Zener < 6V. Avalanche = fatal, Zener = recoverable. Temperature coefficients are opposite.

Breakdown Voltage (V_BK)

  • V_BK depends INVERSELY on doping concentration
  • Minimum doping (1/10⁸) → P, N types → very high V_BK
  • Moderate doping (1/10⁵) → P, N types → medium V_BK
  • Maximum / Heavy doping (1/10² or 1/10³) → P⁺, N⁺ types → very low V_BK

1. Avalanche Breakdown

  • Occurs at > 6V breakdown voltage
  • Moderate or minimum doping
  • Mechanism: IMPACT IONIZATION — minority charge carriers collide with ions of depletion region → creates more ions/holes/electrons → chain reaction → large current
  • Large amount of heat released → diode CANNOT RECOVER
  • Avalanche breakdown is FATAL in nature
  • Positive temperature coefficient: T ↑ → breakdown voltage ↑

2. Zener Breakdown

  • Occurs at < 6V breakdown voltage
  • Heavy doping required
  • Mechanism: High electric field causes RUPTURE of covalent bonds → generates carriers
  • E ↑ and W (depletion width) ↓ — thin depletion region due to heavy doping
  • Covalent bond ruptured by high E can be RECOVERED → diode can be used again
  • Zener breakdown is NON-FATAL
  • Negative temperature coefficient: T ↑ → breakdown voltage ↓ (breaks earlier)
★ NOTE: For forward bias: both Avalanche and Zener diodes act similarly — the difference is only in reverse bias breakdown behaviour.

13. Zener Diode & Applications

Zener Diode as Voltage Regulator

  • Zener Diode is used as a VOLTAGE REGULATOR
  • Avalanche Diode CANNOT be used as voltage regulator (fatal breakdown)
  • Symbol: ⊣ (with bent/Z-shaped cathode line)
  • Works in reverse bias breakdown region — maintains constant voltage across load
Why Zener works as regulator: In breakdown, even if current changes, voltage across zener stays nearly constant = V_Z. This regulated output is used as a stable reference voltage.

Application: Diodes as Rectifiers

  • Diodes used in rectifier circuits to convert AC → DC
  • Half-wave rectifier: One diode — uses only half of AC cycle
  • Full-wave rectifier: Uses both halves of AC cycle (bridge or centre-tap circuit)

14. Quick Reference — Key Formulas

Constants

Carrier Concentration

Intrinsic:         nₑ = nₚ = nᵢ

N-type minority:   nₚ = nᵢ² / ND
P-type minority:   nₑ = nᵢ² / NA
Mobility & Conductivity

Conductivity (general):   σ = nₑqμₑ + nₕqμₕ

Conductivity (N-type):    σ ≈ ND·q·μₑ
Conductivity (P-type):    σ ≈ NA·q·μₕ

Resistivity:      ρ = 1/σ
Current density:  J = σE = I/A

Static resistance:   r_DC = V_D / I_D
Dynamic resistance:  r_ac = n·V_T / I_D    (1Ω to 50Ω; always r_ac < r_DC)

Temperature:     ΔI_S / ΔT → doubles every 10°C
                 ΔV_D / ΔT = –2.5 mV/°C  (forward bias)
